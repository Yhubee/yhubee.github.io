Why This Work

The motivations behind my research

My commitment to Human–Centered AI was shaped by moments where I saw how easily intelligent systems can influence human decision-making—quietly, subtly, and without users realizing it.

One defining moment was a research session with an autistic user, "Maria." She relied on predictable patterns in digital tools. Over multiple interactions, I watched her gradually defer to the AI—not from earned trust, but from cognitive fatigue and perceived system authority.

AI systems don't need to be dominant to displace human judgment; they only need to be confident in the presence of user uncertainty.

This crystallized my purpose: to design and study AI systems that reinforce autonomy, preserve self-determination, and strengthen—not overshadow—human reasoning.

The Problem

In EdTech, I saw how opaque AI recommendations can influence educational pathways, career trajectories, and personal identity. Students accept suggestions because they feel lost or intimidated. In these cases:

  • AI becomes a silent decision-maker
  • Users mistake confidence for correctness
  • Human agency is gradually displaced

Ethical UX is only the first step. What's missing: rigorous methods for measuring autonomy drift, trust calibration, and over-reliance over time.

What I Want AI to Uphold

Dignity

Respecting every user's worth, regardless of background.

Agency

Preserving users' ability to make informed decisions.

Fairness

Treating all users equitably across contexts.

Interpretability

Making AI behavior understandable.

The communities I've worked with—girls in Abuja, students in Benin City, vulnerable users in London—reinforce my belief that technology must adapt to the human, not the other way around.

My Vision

A future where users like Maria can interact with AI that strengthens—rather than diminishes—their confidence in their own judgment.
